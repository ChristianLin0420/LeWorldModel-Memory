Short-context latent world models can predict local dynamics yet discard
evidence needed later. We separate four questions: is an old cue required,
encoded while visible, retained beyond raw context, and used in execution? We
attach parameter-matched persistent modules, or \emph{carriers}, to frozen
released LeWorldModel hosts and train them on next-feature prediction without
task labels. In matched four-color Reacher and PushT tasks, cue age is 4, 8, or
15. At age 15, fixed-trust leads state-space by 6.25 and 6.04 percentage points
(pp); their cross-host difference is $-0.21$ pp (90\% interval
$[-4.83,4.42]$ pp) and meets the registered $\pm5$-pp equivalence criterion.
Every learned arm's linear-readout accuracy declines with age. Predeclared
DINO-WM PushT and PointMaze extensions test the same intervention on
spatial-token hosts with a short-context reset and an external execution test.
On PushT, only state-space clears both age-15 contrasts for six-way binding; on
PointMaze, every learned carrier exceeds no state and a distribution-shifting
context reset, and raises fixed-controller success by up to 53.6 pp over no
state.
The contribution is a protocol separating retention from local prediction,
decodability, and native planning---not a universally superior architecture.
