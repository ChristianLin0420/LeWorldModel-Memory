Short-context latent world models can predict local dynamics yet discard
evidence needed later. We separate four questions: is an old cue required,
encoded while visible, retained beyond raw context, and used in execution? We
attach parameter-matched persistent modules, or \emph{carriers}, to frozen
released LeWorldModel hosts and train them on next-feature prediction without
task labels. In matched four-color Reacher and PushT tasks, the registered
fixed-trust--state-space host interaction lies inside its specified
$\pm5$-percentage-point equivalence region, while every learned arm becomes
less readable with cue age. DINO-WM
PushT reveals a different failure: cues can remain decodable inside a carrier
while becoming poorly exposed by the frozen predictor; only state-space clears
both age-15 controls for six-way binding. On PointMaze, every learned carrier
clears both no-state and distribution-shifting context-reset controls, and
fixed-controller success rises by up to 53.6 pp over no state.
The contribution is a protocol separating retention from local prediction,
decodability, and native planning---not a universally superior architecture.
