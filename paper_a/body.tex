\section{Introduction}

Latent world models compress visual observations and predict how their latent
state changes under action.  This makes planning efficient, but it leaves a
basic question unanswered: when a decision depends on an observation that has
already left the model's input window, does the model still retain it?  Low
next-step prediction error does not answer that question.  A target can be
irrelevant to local dynamics yet decisive for a later goal, and a predictor can
fit every intervening frame while discarding it.

Three common evaluations blur this distinction.  Longer context exposes more
raw observations but is not persistent episode state.  Longer imagination
extrapolates farther from its current anchor but cannot recover evidence absent
from that anchor.  A probe can decode a state without showing that a decision
uses it.  Recurrent and state-space models, retrieval, and geometric memories
provide useful mechanisms \citep{r_dreamerv3,r_r2i,r_s4wm,r_memworld}; the
interface alone does not show which old evidence survives or affects behavior.

We introduce a controlled audit with separately predeclared, fail-closed
branches that test four ordered claims.  First, the old cue must be necessary:
paired episodes share their late observations and actions but require different
answers.  Second, the frozen host must encode the cue while it is visible.
Third, a small persistent state module attached to the frozen feature stream---a
\emph{carrier}---must retain the cue after it leaves raw context.  Fourth, the
same fixed consumer applied to every carrier arm must convert retained state
into a changed executed outcome.  Each stronger test runs only after its
prerequisites pass.  Before each branch begins, its thresholds, endpoints, seed
grid, and bootstrap rule are locked; a failed branch stops instead of being
resplit or relaxed.

For each admitted branch, the intervention freezes a released LeWorldModel
(LeWM) \citep{r_lewm} or DINO-WM \citep{r_dinowm} checkpoint and trains only a
parameter-matched causal carrier.  Labels never enter carrier training: every
learned arm optimizes the same frozen-host next-feature objective.  The design
compares no persistent state, GRU, LSTM, diagonal state-space, and fixed-trust
predict--correct carriers.  Reads occur before the decision observation, and
the DINO-WM protocols additionally compare full-episode state with the same
carrier reset to the legal three-frame context.  Any retention claim therefore
concerns the frozen-host--carrier composition, not the released checkpoint in
isolation or its native planner.

Our controlled LeWM study asks whether an earlier heterogeneous-task ranking
reversal is reproduced when cue semantics and age are matched.  It is not: the
age-15 fixed-trust-minus-state-space contrasts on Reacher and PushT meet the
registered $\pm5$-percentage-point (pp) cross-host equivalence criterion, while
every learned arm's linear-readout accuracy declines with cue age.  This result
changes the ranking interpretation but does not identify which unmatched
factor produced the earlier reversal; checkpoint, environment, and training
distribution still differ.

The DINO-WM studies show why the full ladder matters.  On PushT, state-space
and fixed-trust carriers preserve highly decodable internal state, yet little
is linearly readable after the frozen predictor at long age; only state-space
clears both age-15 contrasts for six-way binding.  On PointMaze, all four
learned carriers exceed no state and a distribution-shifting context reset,
and one shared external consumer turns full-state reads into resolved
navigation gains.  The same carrier interface therefore supports strong,
weak, and bottlenecked memory depending on the host--task composition.

We make three contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \setlength\itemsep{1pt}
  \item an ordered, fail-closed protocol that distinguishes necessity,
        cue-time encoding, persistent retention, and executed use;
  \item a cue-and-age-matched cross-host experiment in which the earlier
        ranking reversal is not reproduced, together with complete age curves
        rather than a single-delay score; and
  \item completed extensions to frozen DINO-WM PushT and PointMaze hosts,
        revealing a linear-readout exposure bottleneck and resolved external
        navigation gains under one shared arm-blind consumer.
\end{itemize}
We do not propose a universally superior carrier, and external-consumer results
are not claims about native world-model planning.

\section{What Counts as Remembering?}

\paragraph{Four ordered claims.}
Let a cue be visible early in a real trajectory and absent from the final raw
context.  We call the number of later real observations its \emph{cue age}.
The audit separates:
\textbf{requirement}, tested by cue-only counterfactuals with identical
post-cue inputs;
\textbf{encoding}, supported by a training-only readout at the last visible cue
frame;
\textbf{retention}, supported at a pre-decision read after shortcut probes
remain below their locked ceilings; and
\textbf{use}, supported only when a fixed consumer changes an executed outcome.
``Full state minus no state'' measures the total carrier benefit.
For DINO-WM, ``full state minus context reset'' estimates, under the reset
intervention, the contribution of carrier state accumulated before the legal
three-frame predictor window while holding carrier parameters fixed.

These claims form a ladder, not interchangeable metrics.  An unencoded cue
cannot diagnose memory.  A retained cue need not appear in the frozen
predictor's preferred coordinates.  A linearly readable state need not be used
by a native planner.  Conversely, a small absolute read can still cause a
paired change in a fixed consumer; we report both quantities so that relative
sensitivity is not mistaken for competent control.

\paragraph{Relation to world-model memory.}
LeWM and DINO-WM predict from bounded latent histories
\citep{r_lewm,r_dinowm}, while Fast-LeWM accelerates imagination without adding
state updated by later real observations \citep{r_fastlewm}.  DreamerV3, Recall
to Imagine, and S4WM maintain recurrent or structured dynamics
\citep{r_dreamerv3,r_r2i,r_s4wm}; ``On Memory'' studies recalled content during
imagination \citep{r_onmemory}; and Mem-World retrieves geometry-indexed visual
history \citep{r_memworld}.  We do not introduce another memory family.  We
instead compare carriers through one frozen-host intervention and one common
endpoint.  RC-aux likewise shows that local predictive quality need not track
planning reachability \citep{r_rcaux}; here the mismatch concerns evidence over
real-observation time.

\subsection{Frozen hosts and a common carrier contract}

The released image encoder, action encoder, latent predictor, and output
projection are immutable.  Each learned carrier receives the current frozen
latent, the intervening action block, and its previous episode state.  Its
read projection is initialized at zero, so every arm begins at the released
host.  GRU, LSTM, diagonal state-space, and fixed-trust carriers are matched in
trainable capacity within each host; optimizer, schedule, data, objective,
fusion point, and state timing are shared.  Fixed-trust uses a channelwise
predicted state and learned input-independent correction trust.  It is not
privileged in the analysis: the protocol, rather than that carrier, is the
methodological contribution.

LeWM consumes three 192-dimensional vector latents and a 10-dimensional action
block.  DINO-WM exposes 196 visual patch trajectories of width 384 together
with proprioceptive and action channels.  One parameter-shared carrier is
applied independently to every patch trajectory; only the frozen native
predictor mixes patches.  The DINO primary read is a fixed $1\!\times\!1$,
$2\!\times\!2$, and $4\!\times\!4$ spatial pyramid of its final visual output.
The carrier's pre-observation prior is preserved as a secondary diagnostic.

\subsection{Counterfactual tasks and stopping gates}

Figure~\ref{fig:tasks-v2} shows the three audit settings.  The completed LeWM
study overlays the same four-color square on Reacher and PushT, with all 16
color--location combinations exactly balanced.  The registered DINO-WM PushT
protocol uses four-way token and six-way ordered-binding tasks on its native
bank; their cue construction and overlay contract appear in
Appendix~\ref{app:dinowm-carrier}.  The PointMaze protocol repeats each native
base window under four transient mini-map goals.  In the matched LeWM study the
cue moves to yield ages 4, 8, and 15 at a fixed endpoint; the DINO-WM protocols
place it at observations 1--3 and move the read to those same ages.  Within
each task, counterfactual variants share every post-cue pixel, action, simulator
state, and endpoint.

Cue-time balanced accuracy must be at least .750; the matched LeWM and
PointMaze protocols additionally require every class recall to reach .700.
Late-input ceilings are chance plus .05: .300 for four-way tasks and .217 for
six-way binding.  A pixel audit requires zero changes outside the declared cue
mask.  Frozen host hashes are checked around every cell.  Failure of any gate
blocks carrier training.  PointMaze also requires its simulator-controller gate
before carrier training, so a failed oracle stops both retention and execution.
Two earlier attempts to create a broader matched suite stopped at their
declared gates and are retained rather than silently discarded.  Their
deterministic selection rule retained color and exact-balanced location; only
then did we construct fresh banks and lock the carrier grid, endpoint,
interaction, and inference before observing any new carrier outcome.  The
color-only comparison is therefore admission-informed rather than a fresh
confirmatory test of target selection.

\subsection{Endpoints and inference}

Readouts are standardized multinomial logistic regressions trained only on the
training bank and evaluated once on held-out episodes.  The matched LeWM study
uses 1,200/480 disjoint episodes per host, five carrier seeds, and a separate
readout at each age.  Its 20,000-draw paired bootstrap jointly resamples carrier
seeds across hosts, independently resamples episodes within host, and stratifies
by the 16 color--location combinations.  The registered interaction is the
PushT-minus-Reacher difference in the age-15 fixed-trust-minus-state-space
contrast.  Equivalence uses a two-one-sided test with a $\pm5$-pp margin, hence
the 90\% interval; the 95\% interval answers the distinct nonzero-effect
question.

DINO-WM PushT uses 1,200/480 native episodes and five seeds for each task
and carrier; its paired bootstrap resamples matched seeds and class-stratified
held-out episodes.  PointMaze uses 300/120 native base windows, expands
each under all four labels, and cluster-resamples intact four-label sets by
native episode as well as matched carrier seed.  No-state copies are
deterministic alignment artifacts and count as one independent model.  The
protocols preserve absolute accuracy, full-minus-no-state, full-minus-reset,
validation predictions, next-feature error, and per-seed arrays; no
heterogeneous score is pooled across hosts.

\section{Matched Cue and Age Do Not Reproduce the Earlier Ranking Reversal}

The motivating LeWM comparison used different cues and delays in Reacher and
PushT and appeared to reverse the ranking of fixed-trust and state-space
carriers.  To test whether that ranking is reproduced under control, we use one
four-way color target on both hosts, balance cue location as an exact nuisance,
and move the cue to ages 4, 8, and 15 while holding each episode endpoint fixed.
A single age-balanced carrier is trained per host, arm, and seed.  All
admissions are recomputed on fresh banks; metrics from the two stopped screens
select the target but never enter this study's inference.

Figure~\ref{fig:matched-v2} gives the complete result.  At age 4, state-space
and fixed-trust readout accuracies are 81.9\% and 77.2\% on Reacher, and 49.6\%
and 60.3\% on PushT.  By age 15 they fall to 35.9\%/42.1\% and
28.1\%/34.2\%.  Every learned carrier has a positive, interval-resolved paired
readout decline from age 4 to 15 on both hosts: Reacher declines range from
3.63 pp (GRU) to 46.04 pp (state-space), and PushT declines from 5.29 pp (LSTM)
to 26.13 pp (fixed-trust).  The no-state read is invariant by construction
(23.1\% Reacher; 25.6\% PushT).

The age-15 fixed-trust-minus-state-space contrast is $+6.25$ pp
$[2.00,10.58]$ on Reacher and $+6.04$ pp $[2.54,9.63]$ on PushT.  Their
registered cross-host interaction is $-0.21$ pp.  Its 90\% interval
$[-4.83,4.42]$ pp lies wholly inside the $\pm5$-pp equivalence region, whereas
its 95\% interval $[-5.75,5.29]$ pp overlaps both equivalence boundaries and
zero.  Under the registered resampling model, this specific ranking contrast
therefore meets the predeclared equivalence criterion; this is neither proof
that the hosts are identical nor universal architecture equivalence.
Per-location summaries retain the exact nuisance balance, and the appendix
reports the weakest-location result for every cell.

The registered ranking interaction from the unmatched study is not reproduced
after matching cue semantics and age.  This does not identify the cause of the
earlier reversal: the released checkpoints still differ in environment,
background, dynamics, and training distribution.  The supported conclusion is
narrower: carrier rankings from unmatched tasks cannot be attributed to
architecture alone, and a single delayed endpoint hides substantial
arm-specific age sensitivity under this training contract.

\section{DINO-WM PushT Separates Internal and Host-Exposed Memory}

We next transfer the intervention from vector-latent LeWM to the original
proprioceptive DINO-WM PushT checkpoint.  Figure~\ref{fig:dinowm-carrier}
compares full state with no state and a legal-context reset as cue age grows.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_mem_dinowm_carrier.pdf}
\caption{Persistent carriers on frozen DINO-WM PushT.  Left: balanced accuracy
of the frozen-predictor output; dashed lines mark chance.  Middle/right: paired
full-state gains over no state and legal-context reset.  Whiskers are 95\%
matched-seed by class-stratified episode bootstrap intervals (20,000 draws).
Positive intervals indicate total carrier effect or dependence on state before
the reset context, respectively.}
\label{fig:dinowm-carrier}
\end{figure}

One parameter-shared carrier processes each of 196 patch trajectories; only the
frozen predictor mixes patches.  The four learned arms have
298,368--299,632 parameters and train without labels on 1,200 native episodes,
with five seeds and 480 held-out episodes.  The primary read spatially pools
the frozen predictor output.  Full minus no state measures total carrier
effect; full minus a three-frame reset estimates dependence on state
accumulated before the legal local context.

Token recall separates the carriers sharply (Figure~\ref{fig:dinowm-carrier}).
At age 4, fixed-trust and state-space reach 68.2\% and 53.5\%, versus 25.4\%
without state; their paired gains over no state are $+42.7$ pp
$[38.2,47.2]$ and $+28.1$ pp $[23.8,32.5]$, and both reset contrasts resolve.
At age 8, their accuracies are 45.6\% and 37.4\%, with positive intervals
against both controls.  By age 15 they fall to 29.3\% and 28.5\%.  Gains over
no state remain resolved ($+6.0$ pp $[3.1,8.8]$ and $+5.1$ pp
$[2.0,8.3]$), but neither reset interval excludes zero.  GRU and LSTM remain
near chance and resolve positively against neither control at any token age.

Six-way binding is harder.  At age 4, state-space/fixed-trust reach
30.9\%/28.1\% (chance 16.7\%) and beat no state by $+14.9$/$+12.0$ pp and
reset by $+14.3$/$+11.4$ pp.  Both comparisons remain positive at age 8.
At age 15, only state-space clears both contrasts: 20.3\%, $+4.7$ pp
$[1.7,7.8]$ over no state, and $+4.0$ pp $[0.5,7.8]$ over reset.
Fixed-trust's no-state interval touches zero, and GRU is resolved below reset,
not evidence of retention.  The state-space absolute interval overlaps chance
while its paired intervals are positive, illustrating the value of matched
counterfactual comparisons.

The secondary carrier-prior read localizes a linear-readout exposure gap.  At age 15, prior
accuracy remains 99.2\%/81.2\% for fixed-trust/state-space on token recall and
97.2\%/64.2\% on binding, far above the corresponding frozen outputs.
Next-feature MSE spans only .0466--.0598 and does not rank semantic recall:
GRU/LSTM often minimize it while showing no gain.  Thus cue identity can remain
linearly accessible inside a carrier yet fail to appear in the frozen host read.  This
native-bank extension supports protocol and carrier portability to one
DINO-WM host; it is not a matched numerical comparison with LeWM, evidence for
native-planner use, or a claim that the released checkpoint itself is
persistent.

\section{Retained PointMaze Goals Drive External Navigation}

PointMaze tests a different DINO-WM environment and MuJoCo execution.
Figure~\ref{fig:pointmaze} connects the registered retention contrasts to a
shared external goal consumer and fixed waypoint controller.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_mem_pointmaze.pdf}
\caption{PointMaze retention and external use.  (a) Balanced accuracy of the
primary frozen-predictor read; chance and no state are 25\%.  (b,c) Paired
full-state gains over no state and legal-context reset.  (d) Age-15 executed
success after a shared arm-blind consumer selects a goal for the fixed waypoint
controller; stars have positive 95\% lower bounds versus both no state and
random goals.  Intervals use 20,000 matched-seed by native-episode cluster
draws; oracle success lies off axis.}
\label{fig:pointmaze}
\end{figure}

We repeat 300/120 disjoint native base windows under four cue-only mini-map
goals, yielding 1,200/480 sequences with identical post-cue pixels, actions,
proprioception, and simulator states.  Cue accuracy and minimum class recall
are 100.0\%; visual, action-only, and proprio-only shortcuts are 25.0\%, below
the .300 ceiling.  The controller gate also passes: 95.6\% oracle success,
93.3\% for the weakest goal, zero off-diagonal false success, and exact reset
replay.

All four learned carriers resolve against no state and context reset at every age
(Figure~\ref{fig:pointmaze}).  At ages 4/8/15, GRU reads
53.0/39.2/35.2\%, LSTM 89.0/67.2/53.5\%, state-space
99.8/95.2/76.0\%, and fixed-trust 100.0/98.8/86.6\%, while no state and
the legal-context reset remain at 25.0\%.  Age-15 gains over no state are
$+10.2$, $+28.5$, $+51.0$, and $+61.6$ pp; reset point estimates are the
same.  Even the smallest reset gain, GRU's $+10.2$ pp $[7.6,13.0]$, is
resolved.  The complete 24 learned-arm contrasts all exclude zero, although
each arm still declines with age.  Reset is much more disruptive for
state-space and fixed-trust: their reset/full next-visual-MSE ratios are
31.4--38.7 and 32.8--39.1 across ages (versus 1.15--1.38 for GRU and
1.85--2.40 for LSTM).  Full-minus-reset therefore identifies dependence under
this intervention, not an unconfounded amount of memory.  Carrier-prior accuracy remains
99.6--100.0\% at age 15, again separating internal retention from how much the
frozen predictor exposes.

For use, one shared arm-blind multinomial consumer per seed is fitted to pooled
full-state training features from every arm.  At age 15 its goal accuracy is
31.7/50.9/78.4/81.5\% for GRU/LSTM/state-space/fixed-trust.  Executing those
goals with the released waypoint controller yields
30.2/48.4/74.5/77.5\% success, versus 23.9\% with no state and 22.4\% for
realized random goals.  Gains over no state are $+6.2$ pp $[4.1,8.5]$,
$+24.5$ pp $[17.2,31.8]$, $+50.5$ pp $[44.2,56.0]$, and $+53.6$ pp
$[49.5,57.4]$; all also resolve over random.  An independent implementation
refits all five consumers and exactly reproduces their predictions.  This is
external memory-conditioned goal selection under one frozen checkpoint and a
fixed controller, not an executed-reset comparison or native DINO-WM planning.

\section{Cross-Study Analysis: Memory Is an Interface Property}

\paragraph{Age changes both level and ranking.}
Every task shows forgetting, but not at a common rate.  To compare shapes
without pooling incompatible banks, consider within-task gain over that task's
own no-state arm.  On DINO-WM PushT, fixed-trust token gain falls from 42.7 pp
at age 4 to 6.0 pp at age 15; state-space falls from 28.1 to 5.1 pp.  Their
binding gains fall from 12.0 to 2.0 pp and from 14.9 to 4.7 pp.  PointMaze is
more durable under the same age schedule: fixed-trust retains 61.6 of its
initial 75.0 pp gain, and state-space retains 51.0 of 74.8 pp.  LSTM and GRU
retain 28.5 of 64.0 pp and 10.2 of 28.0 pp.  These are descriptive within-task
ratios, not cross-bank effect estimates, but they show why one terminal delay
is insufficient: an arm can lead early, converge later, or clear the reset
control on one target but not another.

\paragraph{The carrier and frozen host form two memory stages.}
The DINO protocols read both the carrier's pre-observation prior and the frozen
predictor output.  Their separation is large and systematic.  At PushT age 15,
fixed-trust retains 99.2\% token and 97.2\% binding accuracy internally, yet the
host-exposed reads are 29.3\% and 17.6\%.  State-space shows the same pattern at
81.2\%/64.2\% internally versus 28.5\%/20.3\% after the host.  PointMaze priors
remain 99.6--100.0\% for every learned arm, while host-exposed accuracy spans
35.2--86.6\%.  Thus a low endpoint can arise because state was not retained,
because the frozen fusion/predictor does not expose it, or both.  The prior is
not a substitute endpoint---a consumer attached there changes the interface---
but it prevents these failure locations from being conflated.

\paragraph{Local predictive fit does not order semantic memory.}
On DINO-WM PushT, full-state next-feature MSE lies in a narrow
.0466--.0598 range, and GRU/LSTM often attain the lowest values while their
semantic output remains near chance.  The PointMaze ordering is similarly
misaligned at age 15: GRU has lower mean visual MSE than state-space or
fixed-trust, yet its goal read is 35.2\% rather than 76.0\% or 86.6\%.
This is expected from the objective: cue identity can be irrelevant to local
feature prediction after the cue disappears.  It also explains why we treat
next-feature loss as a shared training contract and health diagnostic, not a
memory metric.

\paragraph{Execution identifies a consumer-specific use link.}
On the PointMaze deck, goal accuracy and executed success move together:
31.7/50.9/78.4/81.5\% goal accuracy becomes
30.2/48.4/74.5/77.5\% success.  The 1.5--4.0 pp conversion loss is much smaller
than the 6.2--53.6 pp gains over no state, while the oracle controller reaches
95.6\%.  For this fixed consumer and controller, retained goal information is
therefore a major limiting factor and changing it changes behavior.  The result
does not imply that a different consumer, native planner, or end-to-end system
would use the same coordinates.  Together, the studies locate memory in the
composition of target, carrier dynamics, frozen exposure interface, and
consumer---not in an architecture name alone.

\section{What the Audit Can and Cannot Localize}

The same intervention produces three qualitatively different diagnoses.  The
matched LeWM ranking interaction is not reproduced, although every learned arm
loses readable information with age.  On DINO-WM PushT, carrier priors remain
highly decodable while frozen-predictor reads approach chance, and only
state-space clears both age-15 contrasts on binding.  On PointMaze, every arm
exceeds no state and a disruptive reset read, and the same external consumer
converts full-state reads into simulated navigation success.  These outcomes are inconsistent with a universal
carrier winner or with next-feature loss as a sufficient memory score.

Full-versus-reset estimates, under that intervention, the contribution of state
accumulated before the native context rather than extra parameters acting only
on the final three frames.  Prior-versus-output gaps localize a carrier-to-host
exposure bottleneck.  External execution establishes that a fixed consumer can
use the representation to change outcomes.  None proves that a released native
planner contains or uses persistent memory, nor does the study isolate which
host, task, or training factor explains the differences.

Legacy controls sharpen this boundary.  On Reacher, a 56-observation raw
context makes the cue readable while the trained predictor output stays near
chance; in this host, local next-latent fit therefore does not guarantee
semantic exposure (Appendix~\ref{app:rollout}).  Learned rollout cohorts beat
copy-last and respond to shuffled actions.  That pattern is inconsistent with
those two trivial baselines on these cohorts, but their anchor occurs after the
old cue (Appendix~\ref{app:rollout}).  Two label-free repairs optimize their
auxiliary targets yet fail their registered support criteria
(Appendix~\ref{app:repair}).  These are localization results, not evidence that
one alternative objective would universally repair memory.

\paragraph{Implications for memory design and evaluation.}
The actionable result is a decomposition, not a carrier ranking.  Local loss
can prefer lower MSE and worse recall, so retention needs an incentive for old
task-relevant evidence.  Prior--output gaps show that the host also needs an
exposure interface.  Fixed-endpoint age sweeps reveal transitions hidden by one
delay, while use must name its consumer: PointMaze supports an external one,
not a native planner.  We therefore report no-state, context-reset,
internal-prior, host-output, and executed-use views instead of one pooled score.

\section{Limitations}

The study covers two LeWM checkpoints and two DINO-WM checkpoints, but its
targets remain controlled categorical cues rather than naturally occurring
long-horizon goals.  The matched LeWM banks do not hold checkpoint training
distribution or environment fixed, and the native DINO-WM banks are not
bank-matched to LeWM, so their absolute scores must not be pooled.  Parameter
matching does not equal optimization matching, and freezing strengthens causal
attribution while excluding end-to-end co-adaptation.  Linear readouts measure
accessible information, not native use.  The PointMaze consumer is an external
goal selector followed by a released controller; it neither tests an executed
reset arm nor modifies the native DINO-WM planner.  Five carrier seeds and
finite held-out banks leave some rankings unresolved, so intervals and seed
arrays are retained rather than reduced to winner labels.
The reset itself is a distributional intervention.  For state-space and
fixed-trust, reset/full next-feature MSE reaches 3.85--8.25 on PushT and
31.4--39.1 on PointMaze; a full-minus-reset contrast therefore identifies
dependence on pre-context state under that reset, not an unconfounded quantity
of memory.  The no-state and external-execution contrasts do not share this
reset caveat.  Ages stop at 15 real observations; multiple simultaneous items,
interference, retrieval after longer delays, and end-to-end learned consumers
remain outside the present claim boundary.

\section{Reproducibility and Conclusion}

Every formal protocol is hash-locked before outcomes.  Runners reject wrong
GPUs, missing cells, schema drift, altered frozen weights, and nonfinite metrics;
cells commit atomically.  Official verifiers check completeness and provenance,
and a separate implementation reconstructs statistics from preserved
predictions.  The appendix reports every cell, stopped branch, amendment,
bootstrap unit, and claim boundary.

Memory is an empirical chain, not a consequence of recurrence or local fit.
Matching removes the earlier LeWM ranking interaction; DINO-WM shows that
linear exposure can bottleneck retained state, while PointMaze converts
full-state reads into external success.  We therefore make bounded task-level
claims, not a pooled memory score or universal architecture winner.
